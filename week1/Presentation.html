COURSE INTRODUCTION

Welcome to CYB 236: Web Application Security

What is Web Application Security?

The practice of protecting websites, web applications, and web services from security threats that exploit vulnerabilities in application code, design, or configuration.

Why Web Security Matters:
Web applications handle sensitive data including personal information, financial records, and health data. The average cost of a data breach in 2024 exceeded $4.45 million.

Web Technologies

HTTP protocol, REST APIs, AJAX, XML, JSON, and modern web architectures.

Security Fundamentals

Injection, XSS, CSRF, XXE, and security misconfiguration.

Security Principles

Authentication, authorization, session management, and access control.

Secure Development

Threat modeling, secure coding, security testing, and vulnerability management.

THE ORIGINS OF HACKING

Understanding the historical roots of security

The Origins of Hacking

In the past two decades, hackers have gained more publicity and notoriety than ever before. But hacking is not a concept tied exclusively to the internet.

Key Insight: Hackers have been around since the middle of the 20th century - potentially even earlier depending on how you define "hacking."
To understand how to defend systems, we must first understand how they can be attacked.
- Fundamental principle of security education

Types of Hackers

White Hat Hackers

Ethical hackers who find vulnerabilities to improve security. They work with permission and follow responsible disclosure practices.

Black Hat Hackers

Malicious actors who exploit vulnerabilities for personal gain, theft, or damage without authorization.

Grey Hat Hackers

Operate in the middle - may find vulnerabilities without permission but typically don't have malicious intent.

Security Researchers

Professionals who study systems to identify and help fix security weaknesses through systematic analysis.

HISTORICAL TIMELINE

Major events that shaped cybersecurity

The Enigma Machine

Circa 1930

The Enigma machine used electric-powered mechanical rotors to both encrypt and decrypt text-based messages sent over radio waves.

  • Symmetric Key Encryption: Identical configurations needed to encrypt/decrypt
  • Configuration Complexity: Billions of possible rotor configurations
  • Military Adoption: Used by German military from 1928
Security Lesson:

The Enigma represents the first major "security system" that would later be compromised - demonstrating that even the most complex systems can have vulnerabilities.

The First "Hackers"

1932 - 1933

In September 1932, Polish mathematician Marian Rejewski was provided a stolen Enigma machine. Along with colleagues Jerzy Rozycki and Henryk Zygalski, he reverse-engineered the system.

  • Analyzed patterns in encrypted messages
  • Understood the mechanics of the machine
  • By 1933, decrypting Enigma traffic daily
Why They Were "Hackers":

Much like hackers today, Marian and his team intercepted and reverse-engineered encryption schemes to get access to valuable data.

Alan Turing & The Bombe

Circa 1940

Alan Turing - English mathematician, often called the father of computer science. Worked at Bletchley Park (GC&CS) from September 1938.

  • Polish methods became ineffective (more rotors added)
  • Turing needed a scalable, automated solution
  • Built The Bombe - an electric-powered mechanical device
  • 108 drums rotating at up to 120 RPM
  • Tested ~20,000 configurations in 20 minutes
Known Plaintext Attack (KPA):

Turing realized German military sent daily weather reports in a predictable format. This "known plaintext" made cracking the encryption much easier. This technique is still used by modern hackers.
Impact: Breaking Enigma is estimated to have shortened WWII by 2-4 years, saving millions of lives. The Bombe was one of the first automated hacking tools ever built.

TELEPHONE PHREAKING

The 1950s-1960s: Hacking the phone system

Telephone Phreaking

Circa 1950 - 1960

In the late 1950s, telecoms like AT&T implemented phones that could be automatically routed based on audio signals. This was called tone dialing.

Hobbyists discovered that reproducing identical audio frequencies could manipulate the system. They became known as phreakers.

Joe Engressia

A young boy with perfect pitch who could whistle at 2600 Hz and manipulate phone systems.

Captain Crunch

John Draper discovered toy whistles in Cap'n Crunch cereal produced a 2600 Hz tone.

Blue Boxes

Hardware devices that generated specific frequencies, allowing anyone to exploit the free calling bug.

Phreaking: Legacy & Response

Famous Phreakers:

Steve Wozniak and Steve Jobs (Apple founders) built and sold blue boxes before starting Apple Computer. The hacker mindset of understanding and manipulating systems has driven technological innovation.
The Response - DTMF:

In the 1960s, Dual-Tone Multi-Frequency (DTMF) signaling was introduced. Each key emitted two specific frequencies simultaneously, making replication much harder. This was the first major security development to combat phreakers.
Critical Lesson

Technology designed without considering users with malicious intent will eventually be broken into.

Always consider the worst-case scenario when designing systems deployed at scale.

THE COMPUTER HACKING ERA

The 1980s: Personal computers and the first viruses

The Computer Hacking Era

Circa 1980

1976: Apple released the Apple 1

1982: Commodore 64 released - nearly 500,000 units/month until the early 1990s

First Computer Virus (1983):

Fred Cohen created the very first computer virus. It could copy itself and spread via floppy disk, hidden inside a legitimate program. He later demonstrated that detecting viruses from valid software with algorithms was almost impossible.

The Morris Worm (1988)

Robert Morris deployed the first virus that infected computers outside a research lab.

  • Spread to ~15,000 computers in one day
  • Estimated damage: $10,000,000
  • First convicted hacker in the US
  • Led to creation of CERT

1980s: Legislation & Legacy

Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA) - 1986:
The first major US legislation specifically addressing computer crimes, establishing federal crimes related to computer intrusion.
Modern techniques still mirror those from the 1980s

Scalability

Jumping from one user to another - now targeting web browsers instead of operating systems.

Camouflaging

Hiding malicious code inside legitimate programs - a technique still at the core of modern attacks.

RISE OF THE WORLD WIDE WEB

The 2000s: From documents to applications

The Web 2.0 Revolution

Circa 2000

The early 2000s marked Web 2.0 - websites began storing user-submitted data and modifying functionality based on user input.

The web transformed from a document-sharing platform to an application distribution platform.

This gave birth to social media, blogs, wikis, and media sharing sites.

New Attack Vectors Emerge:

  • SQL Injection - Late 1990s
  • Cross-Site Scripting (XSS) - First documented 2000
  • Denial of Service (DoS) - Shut down Yahoo!, Amazon, eBay
  • Phishing - Rampant by mid-2000s
With servers and networks secured, the user became the perfect target.

Early Web Breaches & OWASP

CardSystems (2005)

40 million credit cards exposed in one of the first major web-based data breaches.

TJX Companies (2007)

94 million customer records stolen through SQL injection vulnerabilities.

OWASP Founded (2001)

The Open Web Application Security Project began providing free security resources to developers.

Key Shift: With Web 2.0, hackers no longer needed to target the server or network directly. They could now exploit the application logic and user trust to gain access to sensitive data.

HACKERS IN THE MODERN ERA

2015 and beyond: Complexity breeds vulnerability

Modern Threat Landscape

Circa 2015+

Today's web applications have hundreds of open source dependencies, integrations with other websites, multiple databases, and are served from multiple servers in multiple locations.

Advanced Persistent Threats (APTs)

Long-term, targeted attacks often sponsored by nation-states.

Ransomware

Attacks that encrypt data and demand payment (WannaCry, NotPetya).

Supply Chain Attacks

Compromising software vendors to attack their customers (SolarWinds 2020).

Bug Bounty Programs

Companies paying ethical hackers to find vulnerabilities (Google, Microsoft, Apple).

Major Modern Breaches

Equifax (2017)

147 million records exposed due to an unpatched Apache Struts vulnerability - a simple fix that wasn't applied.

Capital One (2019)

100 million records stolen through a misconfigured AWS firewall - showing the importance of cloud security.

Log4j (2021)

Critical vulnerability in a widely-used Java logging library affected millions of systems worldwide.

SolarWinds (2020)

Supply chain attack compromised thousands of organizations including US government agencies.

Modern Browser Security

Browsers have become significantly more advanced with new security features:

Same Origin Policy (SOP)

Robust isolation between websites with different origins.

Content Security Policy (CSP)

Allows developers to specify security levels for their applications.

HTTPS/TLS

Encryption for data in transit, making man-in-the-middle attacks very difficult.

Key Takeaway: As a result of browser security improvements, the most successful hackers today target application logic and code vulnerabilities rather than the browser itself. Modern hackers spend most of their time exploiting bugs in web application code, not trying to break the underlying platform.

SUMMARY & KEY TAKEAWAYS

Essential concepts from this topic

Key Takeaways

Security Evolves

From Enigma to cloud computing, security challenges grow with technology

Cat and Mouse

Attackers and defenders continuously adapt to each other's methods

Human Factor

Many breaches stem from human error, not just technical flaws

Defense in Depth

Multiple layers of security are essential for protection

Each new technology comes with its own unique attack surface and vulnerabilities. One way to become an excellent security professional is to always stay up to date on the latest technologies.

Key Terms to Remember

Enigma Machine

WWII-era encryption device using symmetric key cryptography.

Known Plaintext Attack

Cryptographic attack using known input/output pairs to deduce encryption keys.

Phreaking

Early form of hacking targeting telephone systems using audio frequency manipulation.

Morris Worm

First major Internet worm (1988) that led to CERT and the first hacker conviction.

Web 2.0

The shift from static document sharing to interactive, user-driven web applications.

APT

Advanced Persistent Threat - sophisticated, long-term targeted attacks, often nation-state sponsored.
